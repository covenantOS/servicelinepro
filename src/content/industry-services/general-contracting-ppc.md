## Campaign Structure for General Contractors

A well-structured Google Ads campaign for general contractors separates services into distinct campaigns or ad groups. This structure allows us to write specific ad copy for each service, direct clicks to relevant landing pages, and allocate budget based on which services generate the most revenue and profit.

### Service-Specific Campaign Architecture

We build campaigns around your highest-value service lines. A typical general contractor campaign might include these ad groups:

**Kitchen Remodeling** targets keywords like "kitchen remodel contractor," "kitchen renovation company," "kitchen remodeling near me," and "kitchen contractor." The ad copy speaks directly to kitchen projects, references your kitchen portfolio, and sends traffic to a landing page showcasing your kitchen work.

**Bathroom Renovation** targets "bathroom remodel contractor," "bathroom renovation," "master bath remodel," and related terms. Different ad copy. Different landing page. Different portfolio images. The homeowner sees an ad and a landing page that match their exact project.

**Home Additions** targets "room addition contractor," "home addition builder," "house addition company," and "add a room to my house." Home additions are often the highest-value projects a general contractor handles. The landing page for this campaign should emphasize your experience with structural work, permitting, and large-scale projects.

**Whole House Renovation** targets "whole house remodel," "gut renovation contractor," "complete home renovation," and "total home remodel." This captures homeowners with the largest budgets and most complex projects.

**General Contracting** captures broader terms like "general contractor near me," "residential contractor," "licensed general contractor," and "home improvement contractor." These searchers may not have a specific project in mind yet, or they may have multiple projects they want to discuss.

### Google Local Services Ads

Local Services Ads (LSAs) deserve their own strategy. They appear at the very top of search results, above traditional Google Ads, with your business name, reviews, and a Google Guaranteed badge. You pay per lead rather than per click, which shifts the risk from you to Google.

For general contractors, LSAs typically cost $30 to $80 per lead. A lead means someone actually called you or sent a message through the ad. Compare that to traditional Search Ads where you pay per click regardless of whether the click converts. LSAs are particularly effective for general contractors because the Google Guaranteed badge builds trust for high-value projects where homeowners are especially cautious about who they hire.

We recommend running LSAs in parallel with traditional Search Ads. LSAs capture leads from the top of the page. Search Ads capture leads from the middle. Together, they create a dominant presence that pushes competitors down the page.

### Negative Keywords and Waste Elimination

General contractor keywords attract unwanted clicks if you do not actively manage negative keywords. Searches for "general contractor jobs," "general contractor salary," "how to become a general contractor," and "general contractor license" are all informational queries from people who are not looking to hire you. Without negative keywords, you pay for every one of those clicks.

We build extensive negative keyword lists before launch and refine them weekly based on search term reports. This eliminates wasted spend and concentrates your budget on searches from homeowners who actually want to hire a contractor.

Common negative keywords for general contractor campaigns include: jobs, salary, hiring, license, exam, training, school, courses, insurance requirements, and DIY. We also exclude competitor brand names unless you specifically want to target them.

## Landing Page Strategy

Sending Google Ads traffic to your homepage is one of the most expensive mistakes general contractors make. Your homepage serves many purposes and talks to many audiences. A homeowner who clicked an ad for "kitchen remodel contractor" wants to see kitchen remodels. Not your company history. Not your full service list. Kitchen remodels.

We build dedicated landing pages for each major service category in your campaign. Each landing page includes:

A headline that matches the ad copy and the searcher's intent. If the ad says "Kitchen Remodel Contractor," the landing page headline says something like "Kitchen Remodeling by [Company Name]."

Portfolio images showing completed projects in that category. Real photos of your work, not stock images. Homeowners can tell the difference, and stock photos kill credibility.

A clear call to action above the fold. Phone number, contact form, or both. The homeowner should not have to scroll to find out how to reach you.

Social proof including review excerpts, star rating, license number, insurance information, and any awards or certifications.

Project details including typical scope, timeline, materials used, and price ranges. General contractors often hesitate to publish pricing, but providing a range like "$35,000 to $80,000 for a typical kitchen remodel" pre-qualifies leads and sets expectations.

## Common Google Ads Mistakes General Contractors Make

**Running one campaign for everything.** Lumping "kitchen remodel," "home addition," "bathroom renovation," and "general contractor" into a single campaign means one ad copy speaks to all searches. It cannot. Specificity wins in Google Ads.

**Sending traffic to the homepage.** Your homepage is not a landing page. It serves too many purposes to convert paid traffic effectively. Build dedicated pages for each service.

**Not tracking phone calls.** Most general contractor leads come through phone calls, not form fills. If you are not tracking calls with dynamic number insertion, you have no idea which keywords and ads generate your best leads.

**Setting it and forgetting it.** Google Ads requires active management. Bids change. Competitors enter and exit the auction. New search terms emerge. Negative keywords need updating. A campaign that runs on autopilot bleeds money within weeks.

**Targeting too broad a geography.** General contractors often set a 50-mile radius and bid the same amount for every location. But a lead from a wealthy suburb with $500,000 homes is worth more than a lead from an area with $150,000 starter homes. We adjust bids by location to concentrate spend where your highest-value customers live.
