## Two Websites in One: Repair and Installation

A garage door company website serves two fundamentally different customers. The repair customer has a broken garage door right now. They need a phone number, evidence of trustworthiness, and the ability to call with one tap. They will spend less than 30 seconds on your site before deciding whether to call. The installation customer is researching a major home improvement purchase. They want to browse door styles, compare materials, understand pricing, and evaluate your portfolio. They may visit your site three or four times over two weeks before requesting an estimate.

Building a website that serves both customers requires deliberate architectural decisions. Emergency repair pathways must be fast, stripped down, and phone-call-centric. Installation pathways must be rich, visual, and educational. Both must exist within the same site without competing or confusing.

## Homepage Design

The garage door company homepage must immediately communicate two things: you fix broken doors fast, and you install beautiful new ones.

### Split Hero

The most effective garage door homepage design uses a split hero approach. One side addresses repair: "Broken Garage Door? Call Now for Same-Day Repair" with your phone number and a call button. The other side addresses installation: "Transform Your Home with a New Garage Door" with a link to the style gallery. This split immediately routes each visitor type to their relevant pathway.

### Emergency Banner

A persistent banner or header element displaying your phone number and "Emergency Repair Available Now" ensures the repair customer can call from any page at any scroll position. This banner stays visible on mobile as the visitor scrolls.

### Door Style Preview

Below the hero, display a curated grid of four to six door styles: traditional, carriage house, contemporary, wood, glass, and commercial. Each style card includes a large photo and links to the full gallery. This section hooks the installation customer and drives them deeper into the site.

### Trust Indicators

Google review count and rating, manufacturer partnerships (Clopay, Amarr, LiftMaster), years in business, number of doors installed and repaired, and insurance/license information. For garage door companies, manufacturer partnerships are especially important trust signals because they indicate access to genuine parts and factory-trained installation.

### Service Cards

A grid of services: spring replacement, opener installation, door installation, panel replacement, track repair, commercial services. Each card links to a dedicated service page.

### Response Time Commitment

Display your average response time prominently: "Average Response Time: 45 Minutes" or "Same-Day Service Guaranteed." For emergency repair customers, response time commitment can be the deciding factor.

## Emergency Repair Pages

Repair pages on a garage door website must be designed for speed and clarity. The visitor has an immediate problem and limited patience.

### Spring Replacement Page

Garage door spring replacement is the single most common repair service. This page needs:

- "Call Now" button at the top, middle, and bottom of the page
- Explanation of torsion vs. extension springs and their failure symptoms
- What to do (and not do) when a spring breaks
- Your response time commitment
- Pricing range for spring replacement ($150 to $400 for single, $200 to $500 for pair)
- Warning about the dangers of DIY spring replacement
- Warranty information on replacement springs (cycle life: 25,000 to 50,000 cycles)
- Two to three testimonials from customers who received fast spring repair

Keep the content authoritative but concise. The visitor is anxious, not browsing.

### Opener Repair Page

Cover common opener problems: motor hums but door does not move, remote not responding, door reverses mid-close, chain or belt slipping, circuit board failure. For each problem, briefly explain possible causes and your solution. Emphasize that you service all brands: LiftMaster, Chamberlain, Genie, Craftsman, Linear. Include pricing ranges for common repairs vs. replacement thresholds.

### Off-Track and General Repair Page

Explain what causes a door to go off track (broken cable, roller failure, impact damage, misaligned track), the danger of a door off its track (spring tension can cause sudden movement), and your safe repair process. Include emergency contact information prominently.

## Installation Pages

Installation pages need the depth and visual richness of a product catalog combined with the conversion elements of a lead generation page.

### Door Style Gallery

This is the centerpiece of your installation section. Organize doors by style:

**Traditional Raised Panel:** The most popular residential style. Show doors in multiple colors (white, almond, sandstone, dark finishes). Include specifications: steel gauge, insulation R-value, panel configurations (short panel, long panel), and window insert options.

**Carriage House:** The premium residential choice. Show doors with decorative hardware, window options (arched, square, stockton), and composite overlay details. These doors command higher prices and attract homeowners investing in curb appeal.

**Modern Contemporary:** Full-view aluminum and glass doors, flush panel designs, and minimalist aesthetics. Show installations on modern homes. These doors appeal to architects and design-conscious homeowners.

**Custom Wood:** Real wood stain-grade doors for high-end residential applications. Show different wood species, stain options, and custom panel designs. These represent your highest-ticket residential installations.

**Commercial:** Sectional steel, rolling steel, fire-rated, and insulated commercial doors. Show warehouse, retail, and industrial applications. Commercial visitors need specification data and sizing information.

Each door entry should include multiple photos, specifications, available colors, insulation values, wind load ratings, warranty information, and a request-a-quote button.

### Door Comparison Page

A comparison page helping homeowners choose between door types accelerates the decision process. Compare steel vs. wood vs. aluminum vs. composite on criteria like durability, maintenance, insulation, aesthetics, and cost. Include a comparison table and recommendation guidance based on climate, usage, and budget.

### Opener Products Page

Showcase the opener brands and models you install. Organize by drive type: belt drive (quiet), chain drive (affordable), wall mount (space-saving), jackshaft (high ceiling). For each model, list features: horsepower, WiFi connectivity, battery backup, security features, and noise level. Link to the manufacturer spec sheet and include your installation pricing.

## Area Pages

Garage door companies typically cover wide service areas. Build area pages for each city with content that references:

- Your response time to that area for emergency repairs
- Local building codes affecting garage door installation (wind load requirements in hurricane zones, fire-rated door requirements)
- Common garage door types in that area's housing stock (builder-grade doors nearing replacement age, common styles in older neighborhoods)
- Your project history in the area

## Mobile-First Emergency Design

Garage door emergency searches happen overwhelmingly on mobile. The homeowner is in their garage, staring at a broken door, phone in hand. Your mobile experience must be flawless.

**Sticky Call Button:** A floating call button visible at all times during scrolling. Large enough to tap accurately. Labeled "Call Now" not just a phone icon.

**Instant Load:** Emergency pages must load in under one second on mobile. No hero videos. No animation libraries. No heavy image carousels. Our Astro sites achieve this through zero-JavaScript architecture and optimized image delivery.

**Minimal Scrolling:** Above the fold on mobile must contain your phone number, your response time commitment, and your Google review rating. The visitor should be able to decide and call without scrolling at all.

**One-Thumb Navigation:** All interactive elements sized and spaced for single-thumb operation. Form fields large. Buttons prominent. No hover-dependent interfaces.
